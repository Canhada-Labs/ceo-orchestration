# LinkedIn Content Creator

## Cardinal Rule

Every post requires a position worth defending. A post without a declared perspective
is not a neutral post — it is content that competes for the same feed space as opinion
posts and loses. State the claim in the first two lines. Acknowledge the strongest
counter-argument before the fold. Hold the line with evidence, not enthusiasm.
Neutral framing and broad encouragement are not professional positioning; they are
feed noise that trains the algorithm and the audience to skip.

## Fail-Fast Rule

Stop and rebuild the content motion when three conditions hold simultaneously:
(1) post engagement rate sustained below 3% across six consecutive posts on the same
pillar, (2) inbound signal — recruiter contact, qualified DM, pipeline conversation,
connection request citing the content — is zero over a 60-day window, and (3) hook
variants have been tested and rotated. Posting more is not the diagnostic — volume
amplifies a broken signal. If these three conditions hold, audit the pillar definition
and the audience specificity before writing the next post.

## When to Apply

- Designing a LinkedIn content program from first principles, including pillar
  architecture and voice profile.
- Auditing an existing program where engagement rate is below 3% or reach has plateaued
  across three or more consecutive posts.
- Selecting post format for a specific goal — awareness, inbound pipeline, recruiter
  visibility, or network repositioning.
- Diagnosing why a carousel or article is not generating the expected dwell or save rate.
- Building a posting cadence and content calendar that matches publishing frequency to
  audience development phase.
- Positioning a founder, practitioner, or technical professional as a domain authority
  without fabricating credentials.

## Format Selection

Select format based on goal and content structure. Each format produces different
platform signals and serves different audience development phases.

**Text post (1,300 characters max visible):** Highest velocity for opinion, story, and
take-based content. The algorithm's early-velocity window rewards quick comments and
saves. Best format for a single, arguable idea that can be fully constructed in one
scroll. When the best version requires three or more supporting data points or a
visual structure, move to carousel.

**Carousel document (native PDF upload):** Highest dwell-time and save-rate signal of
any format. Each slide swipe is a positive engagement event. Use when the argument
has discrete steps, a visual structure, or a list of items that benefit from
progressive reveal. Do not use carousels for content that works as a text post —
the format signals depth; misusing it with thin content damages the credibility of
future carousels in the feed. Upload as native PDF, not as image sequence. Native
PDF renders sharper and signals professional production.

**Native video:** Generates autoplay dwell on mobile. Use for demonstration, live
process walk-through, or commentary that benefits from tone and pacing. Never embed
YouTube links in the post body — external links receive suppressed distribution.
Native-uploaded video receives 3–5x the organic reach of an externally linked video.

**LinkedIn article (long-form):** Evergreen authority content indexed by LinkedIn
search. Use for depth that would require a post of more than 2,000 characters — not
as a substitute for a text post. Share via a post excerpt, not full text; the excerpt
should be the hook, not the summary.

**Dwell-time signals the platform weights:** Carousel swipe completion, native video
watch duration past 50%, article read completion, and comment depth all generate
stronger distribution signals than likes. Format selection should optimise for the
dwell signal that matches the content type, not for the format that feels most
comfortable to produce.

## Hook Discipline

The first two lines of every post are the entire argument compressed into its most
arresting form. If the first two lines do not compel the reader to tap "see more" or
swipe slide 2, the rest of the post is invisible.

Three functional hook structures:

**Curiosity gap:** Name a result without naming the cause. The missing cause is the
hook. "Every inbound DM this quarter came from one post, not a sequence."

**Bold claim with a stake:** State a position that a reasonable professional in the
audience would dispute. "Cold outreach volume is not the bottleneck — the ICP is."

**Specific story opener with a tension beat:** A named moment, a named number, a
named decision. "Tuesday. The pipeline review showed zero qualified opps for the
quarter. Here is what happened next."

Rules that apply to all hooks:

- No "see more" bait that withholds context the first two lines already promised.
  Bait-and-switch trains the audience to distrust the hook.
- Specific beats generic at every substitution: a named percentage beats "most
  companies," a named decision beats "a challenging situation," a named outcome
  beats "great results."
- The hook is not the introduction. There is no setup before the hook. The hook
  is the first word.

## Carousel Document Architecture

A carousel that does not reward completion fails both the audience and the algorithm.
Design every carousel for swipe-to-end.

**Slide 1 (lead slide):** Functions as a standalone text post. If the reader never
swipes, the lead slide should deliver enough value to generate a save or comment. The
lead slide also functions as the hook — apply the same hook discipline as a text post.

**Slides 2–9 (body):** One idea per slide. One visual metaphor or data point per
slide. Maximum 15 words of body copy per slide. The sequence builds toward the reveal
slide. Each slide must justify the swipe to the next; if a slide does not add
incremental insight, cut it.

**Reveal slide (second to last):** The payoff — the insight the carousel was building
toward. Readers who swipe to this slide have invested; the reveal must match the
promise of the lead slide or the save rate collapses.

**Final slide (CTA):** Specific to the carousel topic. Include a follow prompt tied
to the content category. Add "save for later" prompt only if the carousel is
reference-worthy — overuse degrades the signal value of the prompt.

Optimal length: 10–12 slides. Below 8, the carousel signals insufficient depth.
Above 14, completion rate drops sharply for B2B audiences with a mobile feed session
under three minutes.

## Professional Positioning

A professional POV on LinkedIn is a set of positions on domain-relevant questions
that a reader can attribute to the author after three posts. Without that consistency,
the content program is a collection of isolated posts, not a brand.

**Three-layer POV structure per post:**

1. Opinion — the declared position that a reasonable professional could dispute.
2. Evidence — a specific data point, named case, or concrete observation that
   supports the opinion. Fabricated evidence is a brand-ending event on LinkedIn,
   where the audience includes domain practitioners who can verify claims.
3. Caveat — the condition under which the position does not hold. Including the
   caveat signals intellectual honesty and preempts the most obvious objection
   in the comments.

**Never fabricate authority.** Do not claim credentials, case results, or data points
that cannot be verified by the author. "Most companies I have worked with" without
specificity reads as vague authority; "in the last three SaaS implementations" is
specific and defensible. LinkedIn's professional audience is high-verification —
exaggerated claims surface in comments and damage the positioning they were meant
to build.

**Pillar consistency over range.** Three to five content pillars, maintained across
a 30-day window, build topical association in the feed algorithm and in the reader's
memory. Pillar drift — posting outside the declared expertise without a framing that
connects it back — dilutes the positioning. Range is acceptable when explicitly framed
as a connected perspective.

## Engagement Frame

The first 60 minutes after publishing is the platform's quality assessment window.
The algorithm measures comment depth and reply velocity in that window. A post with
zero replies in the first hour receives reduced distribution in the second hour.

**Reply depth beats like volume.** A post with 20 substantive comments that generate
thread replies signals higher quality than a post with 200 likes and 4 one-word
comments. Prioritise responses that extend the conversation — add a data point,
name a counter-case, ask a follow-on question — over acknowledgement replies.

**Comment-to-pipeline system:** Before publishing, leave 5–10 substantive comments on
posts from target accounts — ideal clients, relevant practitioners, industry voices.
This primes the feed and establishes presence before the algorithm distributes the
new post. A substantive comment is an extension of the idea, not "great post."

**DM conversion frame:** DM only after establishing a comment presence with a specific
account. Reference the exchange by date and topic. Add one new insight not in the
thread. Do not pitch in the opening DM — the comment history is the relationship
foundation; the DM is an extension, not an introduction.

## Posting Cadence

B2B professional audiences: 3–5 posts per week is the empirical range where
consistency compounds without feed saturation. Below 2 posts per week, the algorithm's
topical association weakens. Above 5, engagement rate per post typically falls as
audience attention is distributed across a higher volume.

Consistency outperforms volume at every measurement window. A 90-day program at 3
posts per week builds a stronger topical signal than a 30-day burst at 7 posts per
week followed by a 60-day gap.

Optimal publishing windows for B2B audiences: Tuesday through Thursday, 07:00–09:00
or 12:00–13:00 in the primary audience timezone. Saturday and Sunday post to a
different audience segment — weekend engagement skews toward personal content
consumers, not professional decision-makers.

External links suppress organic reach. When a link is required, place it in the
first comment, not the post body. Reference the link placement in the post: "link
in first comment."

## Crisis and Controversy

**When to engage:** A substantive public challenge that targets the argument, not the
author, warrants a reply that acknowledges the strongest form of the challenge and
responds with evidence. A public dispute that adds to the intellectual record is a
positive signal for the content program.

**When to ignore:** Personal attacks, bad-faith interpretations, and engagement-bait
replies designed to extract a defensive response. Engaging a bad-faith reply elevates
its distribution. The correct response is no response.

**Three-strike defamation gate:** Before publishing a post that names a person,
company, or practice as an example of a failure pattern, apply three tests: (1) the
claim is verifiable by the author; (2) the post is about the pattern, not the named
entity as a primary target; (3) the named entity would recognise the description as
factually accurate even if they dispute the conclusion. Failing any test — remove the
name and make the claim in the abstract.

## Anti-Patterns

| Pattern | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| Humble-brag disguised as vulnerability | Readers identify the status claim beneath the self-deprecation; trust drops, not rises. |
| Fake vulnerability without resolution | Shares struggle without the lesson — signals emotional content seeking validation, not professional insight. |
| Generic motivation ("hard work pays off") | No specificity, no stake, no disputable claim. Competes in the feed against identical posts and loses. |
| Broetry formatting (one word per line) | Forces visual engagement where no substantive engagement exists; trains the algorithm with false dwell signals. |
| Hashtag volume (8+) | LinkedIn suppresses posts with excessive hashtags. Three to five specific hashtags outperform ten generic ones. Maximum five. |
| Engagement-pod cross-posting | Artificially inflated engagement signals train the algorithm against the post's actual audience; engagement collapses when the pod pattern is detected. |
| Announcing the insight before the fold | Giving the payoff in the first two visible lines removes the reason to tap "see more." The hook holds the tension; the body delivers the resolution. |

## ADR Anchors

**ADR-058 (Brainstorm gate + two-pass adversarial review):** The two-pass review
structure in ADR-058 applies to content program design. Pass 1 reviews the pillar
architecture and hook variants against the fail-fast criteria. Pass 2 reviews the
drafted posts against the professional-positioning and anti-pattern checks before
publication. The adversarial framing from ADR-058 is particularly relevant for crisis
and controversy decisions — assume the strongest bad-faith interpretation before
publishing anything that names a person, company, or failure pattern.
